The Great Enclosure is the largest single ancient structure south of the Sahara — an elliptical outer wall running about 250 meters around, nearly 10 meters high in places and 5.5 meters thick.
It was built mainly in the 13th and 14th centuries, entirely of dry-laid granite blocks in level courses, with no mortar anywhere.
The wall leans slightly inward as it rises for stability, and drainage channels run through its base — quiet engineering that has kept it standing for 600 years.
Inside, a second wall runs parallel to the outer one, creating a narrow high-walled passage that leads toward the Conical Tower.
Within the walls stood clay daga houses — living quarters and a community area, with smaller walls marking off each family's space of kitchen, living huts, and court.
Its exact purpose is uncertain: a royal residence, a compound for religious and administrative life, or a ritual site. What is certain is what it declared — the power and prestige of the rulers who could command such building.
Chevron patterns run along the top of the great outer wall, some of the finest decorative stonework in the city.
