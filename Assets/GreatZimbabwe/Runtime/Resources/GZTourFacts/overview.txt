Great Zimbabwe was the capital of the Kingdom of Zimbabwe, built by the ancestors of the Shona people between roughly 1100 and 1450 AD.
This is the largest ancient stone complex in sub-Saharan Africa. Every wall below is dry-stone granite — no mortar at all — standing for some six centuries on skill and gravity alone.
At its 14th-century height the city held over ten thousand people, most of them living in round clay houses called daga homes spread far beyond the stone walls.
The city was the capital of a true regional state, drawing tribute from more than 150 smaller stone-walled settlements — minor zimbabwes — across the plateau.
Its wealth flowed from great cattle herds and from gold and ivory carried 400 kilometers east to the Indian Ocean, returning as Chinese porcelain, Persian pottery, and glass beads.
The name is Shona — "the house in stone" — and at independence in 1980 the modern nation took the same name: Zimbabwe.
Great Zimbabwe has been a UNESCO World Heritage Site since 1986, and it remains a sacred place for Shona communities to this day.
