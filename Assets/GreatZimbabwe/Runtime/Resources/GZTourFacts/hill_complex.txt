The Hill Complex crowns a granite spur about 80 meters above the valley — the oldest part of the city, home to its rulers from the 11th to the 15th centuries.
This was the royal acropolis: the western enclosure held the residence of successive rulers, while the eastern enclosure served ritual life.
The famous soapstone Zimbabwe Birds stood here in the Eastern Enclosure — six upright steatite posts topped with carved birds were found in this ritual heart of the hill.
The builders worked with the hill, not against it: walls thread between giant natural boulders, so architecture and hill become one structure, with enclosures joined by narrow, partly covered passageways.
Some walls up here rose about nine meters high, flanked by rounded turrets and carved stone monoliths.
A cave on this hill was tied to the ruler's household, and it remains a sacred site for Shona communities today.
