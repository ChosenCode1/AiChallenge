# Visitor Q&A (starter set)

Anticipated questions with answers grounded in `chunks/`. Use for eval of the guide LLM and as few-shot examples. Each answer cites the chunk ids it draws on.

**Q: Who built Great Zimbabwe?**
A: The ancestors of the Shona people — Bantu-speaking Africans of this plateau, whose farming communities had worked this land and its gold and iron for centuries before the walls rose. [gz-chron-01, gz-chron-03]

**Q: When was all this built?**
A: The stone city rose between about 1100 and 1450 AD. Building continued for over 300 years, and the city peaked between 1300 and 1450 as capital of the Kingdom of Zimbabwe. [gz-chron-02]

**Q: How do the walls stand up without cement?**
A: Pure dry-stone skill: granite blocks laid in level courses, interlocked, with walls leaning slightly inward for stability. No mortar anywhere — and they've stood for some six centuries. [gz-arch-01, gz-ge-05]

**Q: Where did all the stone come from?**
A: The granite hills right here. The local granite splits into roughly uniform slabs, giving builders regular blocks without cutting each stone to shape. [gz-arch-02]

**Q: What's the big round tower?**
A: The Conical Tower — about 10 meters of solid coursed granite, no door or chamber. It's symbolic: most likely a giant grain bin in stone, since a Shona ruler showed generosity through his granary. [gz-ge-02]

**Q: What was the Great Enclosure for?**
A: Honestly, it's still an open question — a royal residence, a religious and administrative compound, or a ritual site. What's certain is the message it sent: the sheer power of rulers who could build a 250-meter wall nearly 10 meters high. [gz-ge-04, gz-ge-01]

**Q: Did people live inside these stone walls?**
A: Inside and far beyond them. The walls enclosed round clay daga houses — a family compound had a kitchen, two living huts, and a court. Most of the city's thousands lived in daga homes outside the stone enclosures entirely. [gz-arch-04]

**Q: How many people lived here?**
A: Over ten thousand at the 14th-century peak by UNESCO's account — and some estimates run as high as eighteen or even twenty thousand. Huge for the region in that era. [gz-valley-04]

**Q: Were the walls for defense?**
A: Almost certainly not — many wall segments have gaps and interrupted arcs that make no military sense. They were social architecture: screening rulers from view and displaying power. [gz-arch-05]

**Q: How did the city get rich?**
A: Cattle first — herds were wealth and status. Then control of the gold trade: gold and ivory traveled about 400 km to the coast at Sofala, then by dhow up the Swahili coast through Kilwa toward Cairo, Persia, and China. [gz-econ-01, gz-econ-02]

**Q: What did they get in return?**
A: Chinese porcelain including Ming ware, Persian pottery and faience, coins from Arabia including gold coins from Kilwa, and glass beads — all excavated right here, luxuries flowing to the ruling elite. [gz-econ-03]

**Q: What are the famous bird carvings?**
A: The eight Zimbabwe Birds — soapstone birds of prey mounted on carved monoliths over a meter tall, associated with the Hill Complex's Eastern Enclosure. Likely emblems of royal power: kings as benefactors of their people and intercessors with the ancestors. The bird sits at the heart of the national flag today. [gz-bird-01, gz-bird-02, gz-bird-03, gz-bird-04]

**Q: Why did everyone leave?**
A: No single reason. The land grew tired of feeding a big city, nearby gold thinned out, trade shifted to sites further north, and drought and succession disputes have all been suggested. Power moved north to Mutapa and southwest to Khami — less a collapse than a relocation. [gz-decline-02, gz-decline-03]

**Q: What does "Zimbabwe" mean?**
A: It's Shona — UNESCO renders it "the house in stone," and it has also been translated as "ruler's court." At independence in 1980, the nation took the same name: Zimbabwe. [gz-heritage-01, gz-heritage-02]

**Q: Is this place still important to people today?**
A: Deeply. It's a UNESCO World Heritage Site, the origin of the country's name and flag emblem, and still a sacred place — Shona communities use the site, including the hill cave, for spiritual purposes now. [gz-heritage-03, gz-heritage-04]

**Q: Is this the real terrain?**
A: Yes — you're flying over a real drone survey of the site by Uppsala University researchers, at true scale: about 1.1 by 1.2 kilometers covering the Valley Complex and Great Enclosure, with the Hill Complex at the northern edge. [gz-scene-01, gz-scene-02]
