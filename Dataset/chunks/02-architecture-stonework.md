# Architecture & stonework

## gz-arch-01 — Stone without mortar
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: smarthistory, whe

Every stone wall at Great Zimbabwe is dry-stone construction: granite blocks fitted together with no mortar at all. Stability comes entirely from careful coursing, interlocking, gravity, and walls that lean slightly inward. That these walls have stood for some six centuries without cement is one of the great engineering achievements of the medieval world.

## gz-arch-02 — Where the stone came from
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: smarthistory, whe

The granite was quarried locally, from the very hills the city stands on. The local granite splits into roughly uniform slabs, typically 50–100 cm thick, and builders exploited this to produce blocks that could be laid in level courses without cutting each stone to shape.

## gz-arch-03 — Decorative coursework
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, smarthistory

The masons went beyond function: sections of walling carry chevron (zigzag) and chequered patterns worked directly into the stone courses, and monoliths were carved with elaborate geometric patterns. UNESCO's citation calls the craftsmanship "a unique artistic achievement." The finest decorative work appears on the Great Enclosure's outer wall and on monoliths.

## gz-arch-04 — A city of clay as much as stone
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, smarthistory, libretexts

The stone walls were never roofed buildings — they were enclosures. People lived in *daga* houses: circular homes of clay mixed with granitic sand, on wooden frames, with thatched roofs. Excavated house floors preserve hearths, pot-stands, and raised clay benches for sleeping and sitting. The stone enclosures wrapped around clusters of these houses; a typical family compound held a kitchen, two living huts, and a court. Most of the city's population lived in daga homes outside the walls entirely.

## gz-arch-05 — Not fortresses
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: smarthistory

Despite their scale, the walls were almost certainly not military. Many wall segments have gaps and interrupted arcs that make no defensive sense. The walls' purpose was social and symbolic: screening the rulers from public view, marking status, and projecting the power and prestige of the kings who could command such building.
