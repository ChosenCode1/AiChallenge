# Chronology & builders

## gz-chron-01 — Who built Great Zimbabwe
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, libretexts, smarthistory

Great Zimbabwe was built by the ancestors of the Shona people, a Bantu-speaking people of the Zimbabwe plateau. This is the settled archaeological view: UNESCO records that scientific research has proved the city was founded by a Shona population of the Iron Age. The city was designed, built, and ruled by Africans, and it remains a sacred place for the Shona today.

## gz-chron-02 — When the city lived
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, whe, libretexts

The stone city was built between roughly 1100 and 1450 CE. The site was founded in the 11th century, major stone construction continued for over 300 years, and the city reached its peak between about 1300 and 1450 as the capital of the Kingdom of Zimbabwe. By around 1450–1500 the capital had declined; a description that reached the Portuguese writer João de Barros suggests some people still lived here in the early 1500s.

## gz-chron-03 — Before the city
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: whe

People worked this landscape long before the great walls rose. Shona-speaking farmers migrated onto the plateau from around the 2nd century CE, drawn to temperate grassland where gold could be won from surface deposits and shallow mines. By the 7th–9th centuries, plateau communities were building in stone or mud, making pottery, copper and gold jewelry, and iron tools — and already trading with the coast, as glass beads and seashells found here show. The hill itself carries traces of sparse habitation as far back as the 5th century.

## gz-chron-04 — Mapungubwe before, Mutapa and Khami after
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: libretexts, unesco-whc-364

Great Zimbabwe was the middle chapter of a longer story. Its growth has been linked to the decline of Mapungubwe, an earlier gold-trading kingdom to the south at the Shashe–Limpopo confluence, whose rulers carried their artistic and stone-building traditions north. When Great Zimbabwe itself waned in the 15th century, power moved on again: by tradition prince Nyatsimba Mutota founded the Mutapa state to the north around 1430, and in the southwest the Kingdom of Butua rose at Khami, later absorbed into the Rozwi Empire.

## gz-chron-05 — One of many zimbabwes
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: libretexts, smarthistory

Great Zimbabwe was the greatest of many. The kingdom was composed of over 150 tributary settlements headquartered in their own minor *zimbabwes* — smaller stone-walled centers spread across the plateau — and at the site's cultural zenith an estimated seven comparable states existed in this region. This was the capital of a true regional state, not an isolated monument.
