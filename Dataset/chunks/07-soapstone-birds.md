# The Zimbabwe Birds

## gz-bird-01 — Eight birds in stone
> location: hill_complex · confidence: established · sources: whe, unesco-whc-364

Eight bird sculptures carved from soapstone are the most famous objects ever found at Great Zimbabwe, each mounted on a carved monolith standing over a meter high. The bird resembles no species found in nature — a creature of art and belief rather than field observation.

## gz-bird-02 — Where they stood
> location: hill_complex · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364

Most of the birds are associated with the Eastern Enclosure of the Hill Complex, where six upright steatite posts topped with carved birds were found — the enclosure understood to have served ritual purposes. The spiritual heart of the royal hill is their home ground.

## gz-bird-03 — What the birds mean
> location: hill_complex · confidence: uncertain · sources: smarthistory, whe

The birds are stylized — the pronounced muscular breast and defined talons of a bird of prey, but matching no single species. Scholars have read them as emblems of the power of Shona kings: benefactors to their people and intercessors with their ancestors. Royal power, given wings in stone.

## gz-bird-04 — From ritual enclosure to national flag
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, whe

The Zimbabwe Bird became the emblem of the modern nation: the entire Zimbabwean nation has adopted the steatite bird — which may once have been a royal totem — as its emblem, and it appears at the heart of the national flag today. And at independence in 1980, the country itself took the name Zimbabwe — the Bantu word for these clusters of stone buildings, the word that named the site and the kingdom before it.
