# The Great Enclosure

## gz-ge-01 — The largest ancient structure south of the Sahara
> location: great_enclosure · confidence: established · sources: libretexts, whe, smarthistory

The Great Enclosure is the single largest ancient structure in sub-Saharan Africa. Its elliptical outer wall runs about 250 meters around, stands nearly 10 meters (32 feet) high in places, and is about 5.5 meters thick — all dry-laid granite, no mortar. It was built mainly in the 13th–14th centuries and completed by about 1450.

## gz-ge-02 — The Conical Tower
> location: great_enclosure · confidence: established · sources: whe, unesco-whc-364, smarthistory

Between the outer wall and an inner parallel wall, a narrow passage leads to the Conical Tower — a solid, tapering drum of coursed granite about 10 meters tall and 5 meters across at the base. It has no door, no chamber, no stair: it is symbolic architecture. The favored interpretation is that it represents a grain bin, monumentalized — in Shona tradition a ruler displays generosity through his granary, distributing grain as a sign of protection.

## gz-ge-03 — The parallel passage
> location: great_enclosure · confidence: established · sources: smarthistory, whe

One of the most striking experiences in the Great Enclosure is the narrow corridor formed where an inner wall runs parallel to the great outer wall — a tight, high-walled passage leading toward the Conical Tower. Walking it, the sky narrows to a strip between walls almost ten meters high.

## gz-ge-04 — What went on inside
> location: great_enclosure · confidence: uncertain · sources: unesco-whc-364, smarthistory, whe

Inside the walls were daga-hut living quarters and a community area, with smaller walls marking off family spaces. Its exact purpose is uncertain — readings include the royal residence, a compound for a surplus population's religious and administrative life, or a site for religious ritual. What scholars agree on is what it *meant*: a potent, unmistakable statement of the rulers' prestige and authority.

## gz-ge-05 — Built for stability
> location: great_enclosure · confidence: established · sources: whe

The great wall inclines slightly inward as it rises, adding stability, and regular drainage channels run through its base to keep the level interior dry — small engineering details that help explain how a mortarless wall of this size has stood for some 600 years.
