# Decline

## gz-decline-01 — The city winds down
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, libretexts, whe

Around 1450 the capital declined and was largely given up as the center of power, though a description transmitted to the Portuguese writer João de Barros suggests people still lived here into the early 1500s. The kingdom's energy flowed elsewhere — north to the new Mutapa state, southwest to Khami.

## gz-decline-02 — Why? Several answers, no single one
> location: site_wide · confidence: uncertain · sources: unesco-whc-364, whe, libretexts, smarthistory

No single cause explains the abandonment, and scholars present several together: the surrounding land could no longer feed an overpopulated city (soil exhaustion, deforestation); the gold deposits within reach became depleted; trade declined in favor of sites further north; famine and water shortages induced by climatic change; recurring drought, livestock disease, and disputes over royal succession have all been suggested. Most likely, several of these pressed at once.

## gz-decline-03 — Not a fall — a move
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: libretexts, unesco-whc-364

The end of Great Zimbabwe was less a collapse than a relocation of power. By tradition, prince Nyatsimba Mutota traveled north around 1430 — in search of salt, the story goes — and founded the dynasty that became the Mutapa state, which within a generation eclipsed the old capital. In the southwest, the migration benefited Khami, where the Kingdom of Butua carried the building tradition forward; both successor states were eventually absorbed into the Rozwi Empire.
