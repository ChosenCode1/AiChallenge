# Economy, trade & daily operation

## gz-econ-01 — Cattle first
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: smarthistory, whe, libretexts

Before gold, there was cattle. Herds were the foundation of wealth and status on the plateau, and managing large herds is what gave elites their power. The great herds that fed the city moved seasonally between pastures, managed by the royal court. Cattle wealth is likely what allowed the Shona to expand from subsistence farming into mining and long-distance trade.

## gz-econ-02 — The gold road to the sea
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: smarthistory, whe, libretexts

Great Zimbabwe grew rich by controlling the trade in gold from the plateau's goldfields — mined as near as 40 km (25 miles) from the city — down to the Indian Ocean coast about 400 km away. Gold and ivory traveled to the port of Sofala, then north by dhow along the Swahili coast through Kilwa Kisiwani, reaching markets as distant as Cairo, Persia, and China.

## gz-econ-03 — What came back
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, whe, libretexts

The return trade is written in the ground: excavations at Great Zimbabwe have produced Chinese porcelain — including Ming ware — Persian pottery and carved faience, coins from Arabia including gold coins from Kilwa, and glass beads. These luxury imports flowed to the city's ruling elite as prestige goods.

## gz-econ-04 — Gold in the ground and in the workshop
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: smarthistory, whe, libretexts

Gold wasn't just exported raw. Gold wire jewelry and ornaments, along with iron and copper tools, have been excavated on site. Mining itself evolved from collecting surface deposits to shafts reaching 30 meters underground, and copper was cast into distinctive X-shaped ingots. By some estimates, more than 20 million ounces of gold were taken from the region's ground over the centuries.

## gz-econ-05 — Trade for the few
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: whe, libretexts

The ocean trade sat on top of a local economy of cattle and farming, and its profits ran narrow: the long-distance trade benefited the ruling elite exclusively. There were no public marketplaces — exchange among the nobility ran on barter — and the kingdom drew tribute from over 150 subordinate settlements across the plateau.

## gz-econ-06 — How the city was run
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: whe, libretexts

Society was hierarchical — royalty within the great stone walls, commoners in daga homes around them — and chieftaincy was usually hereditary, with chiefs ruling through accommodation with senior men and subordinate rulers rather than standing armies. Households were patriarchal: status came through cattle and marriage alliances; women farmed, prepared food, and drew water; young men herded, hunted, and were schooled in endurance and custom before taking their adult names.
