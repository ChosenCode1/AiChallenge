# Name & living heritage

## gz-heritage-01 — What "Zimbabwe" means
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, smarthistory, whe

The name is Shona. UNESCO renders *zimbabwe* as "the house in stone"; the word has also been translated as "ruler's court or house" or "judicial center," and clusters of stone buildings were called *zimbabwe* — hence the name of the site and the kingdom. All the readings fit this place: houses of stone, houses of rule.

## gz-heritage-02 — The city that named a country
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: whe, unesco-whc-364, smarthistory

At independence in 1980, the country became Zimbabwe — the same word that had always named this site and its kingdom. The soapstone bird from the Hill Complex flies on the national flag, and the site remains a potent symbol for Zimbabweans broadly. Few countries anywhere carry the name and emblem of a single archaeological site — a measure of what this place means.

## gz-heritage-03 — Still sacred
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, smarthistory

Great Zimbabwe is not a dead monument. UNESCO describes it as a sacred archaeological site still used by contemporary communities for spiritual reasons; the cave on the Hill Complex remains a sacred place for Shona communities today. Visitors walk through a place of active memory and reverence.

## gz-heritage-04 — World Heritage
> location: site_wide · confidence: established · sources: unesco-whc-364, whe

Great Zimbabwe National Monument was inscribed as a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1986, cited as "a unique artistic achievement" and a unique testimony to the Shona civilization of the 11th–15th centuries. The protected property covers almost 800 hectares, about 30 km from the town of Masvingo, at roughly 1,100 m altitude.
