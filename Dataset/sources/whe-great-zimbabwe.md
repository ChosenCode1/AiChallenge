# Great Zimbabwe (World History Encyclopedia)

## Overview

Great Zimbabwe stands as a ruined city near Masvingo in central Zimbabwe, inhabited between approximately 1100 and 1550 CE, with its peak flourishing occurring between 1300 and 1450 CE during the Late Iron Age. The site served as the capital of the Kingdom of Zimbabwe, ruled by the Bantu-speaking Shona people. Built upon a natural citadel and constructed using granite blocks without mortar, this settlement represents a remarkable achievement in African architecture.

## The Zimbabwe Plateau

The Kingdom of Zimbabwe emerged in the Zimbabwe plateau region, positioned between the Limpopo River to the south and the Zambezi River to the north. The Shona, migrating from the 2nd century CE onward, established themselves in this temperate grassland area. Though rainfall remained unpredictable with recurring drought threats, gold was easily acquired from surface deposits and shallow mines throughout the plateau and river tributaries.

Archaeological evidence indicates that by the 7th–9th centuries, communities had established settlement patterns involving stone or mud housing, pottery production, copper and gold jewelry, and iron tools. Trading networks extended to coastal regions, as demonstrated by discovered glass beads and seashells.

## Architectural features

### Hill Complex

The Hill Complex occupied a natural rise standing 80 meters high, providing both ritual significance and defensive advantages. Sparse habitation dating to the 5th century was followed by intensive occupation beginning in the 11th–12th century. The site may have functioned as a burial location for chiefs or as a religious center for ancestor worship. By the mid-13th century, the complex was enclosed by a dry stone wall constructed from locally-sourced granite that could be split into uniform slabs measuring 50–100 centimeters thick.

### Great Enclosure

The dominant feature of Great Zimbabwe, constructed in the 13th–14th centuries, is a massive elliptical stone wall measuring 5.5 meters thick and 9.7 meters high. The wall inclines slightly inwards for added stability and regular channels run through the base to drain the level interior space. The structure incorporates a conical tower standing 10 meters tall and 5 meters wide at its base, built using precise dry-stone granite masonry.

The total circumference reaches 250 meters. While its exact purpose remains unclear, the structure likely functioned as a royal residence with the tower potentially serving as a granary. Most scholars agree the Great Enclosure would have served as a potent symbol of the prestige and authority of the rulers of Great Zimbabwe.

### Valley Ruins

Beyond the primary structures lie numerous individual stone buildings and remains of large circular mud and pole houses, collectively termed the Valley Ruins. These structures, some measuring 10 meters in diameter with thatch roofs reaching approximately 6 meters high, suggest population growth. The entire site sprawls across 1,700 acres with an estimated population of around 18,000 people.

## Government & society

Zimbabwean society operated under a patriarchal structure where male family heads competed for influence and power. Cattle ownership provided the primary means of acquiring status, while the number of wives indicated a man's access to labor. Women performed agricultural work, food preparation, and water collection, while unmarried males hunted, herded animals, and produced clothing.

Tribal chiefs typically represented the wealthiest males, though chieftaincy positions were usually hereditary among the Shona. Chiefs possessed no standing armies, requiring accommodation with senior males and subordinate chiefs under their nominal authority. Evidence of fire destruction at certain sites suggests occasional inter-group conflicts.

Male children of herd-owning families underwent isolated group education teaching hunting skills, physical endurance, and tribal customs, culminating in the adoption of adult names. Girls received similar group instruction preparing them for roles as wives and mothers. Marriage involved the bride moving to her husband's family compound, with her father providing cattle as dowry.

## Trade networks

Great Zimbabwe's prosperity derived significantly from extensive trade connections. The city traded with merchants of the East African coast 400 km (250 miles) away, particularly through Kilwa and its Mozambican outpost of Sofala. These Swahili trading centers flourished as intermediaries for Zimbabwean gold, which was exchanged for luxury imports including Chinese Ming porcelain and carved faience from Persia.

Gold extraction evolved from surface collection to deep mining operations reaching 30 meters depth. Copper, often cast into x-shaped ingots, and ivory supplemented gold exports. This trade benefited exclusively the ruling elite, with no public markets facilitating exchange — transactions occurred through barter among the nobility.

## Art

Systematic looting during the 1890s European colonial period removed most valuable artifacts, with surviving pieces typically lacking contextual information. Finely carved soapstone figures represent the site's most notable artistic achievement, including eight bird representations mounted on monoliths exceeding one meter in height.

The bird is known as the Zimbabwe Bird and does not resemble any bird in nature; it appears on the flag of the country today. Additional sculptures include stylized cattle and female figures. Pottery production included gourd-shaped vessels with hatched triangular decorations, small disks of uncertain function, and miniature hut models, typically featuring graphite coverings and high-quality unglazed finishes.

## Decline & later history

Great Zimbabwe's abandonment during the 15th century likely resulted from multiple factors: exhausted gold deposits, competition from rival states, and potential overpopulation leading to land degradation and deforestation. Droughts may have exacerbated these conditions. Coastal trade connections ceased entirely by century's end.

By the late 15th century, the Shona migrated northward several hundred kilometers to establish the Kingdom of Mutapa. Great Zimbabwe received UNESCO World Heritage Site designation in 1986.
